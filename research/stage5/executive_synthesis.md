# Stage 5 — Executive Synthesis

**Project:** Battery Workforce Whitepaper — *How Many Workers Are Needed for Battery Manufacturing?*
**Organization:** Volta Foundation · Research & Insights · **Date:** June 12, 2026

*This is a research synthesis, not a whitepaper draft. It states what the evidence supports, what must be qualified, and what remains uncertain.*

## Central finding

The honest answer to "how many workers per GWh?" is **not a single number** — it is a small set of scope-defined ranges, and the field's most useful contribution is to say so explicitly. The dominant problem is not missing data but **conflated denominators and boundaries**: figures are quoted without stating whether they cover cells, full supply chain, or induced jobs, and whether the GWh denominator is nameplate or utilized capacity. The paper's defensible core is a **two-tier estimate**: cell+pack ≈ **95–180 workers/GWh**, and full-chain US ≈ **~310 workers/GWh** (an upper bound, from Upjohn's pre-OBBBA scenario). These must never be collapsed into one triangulated headline.

## Strongest defensible claims

- **US cell-manufacturing-tier employment ≈ 60,400** (BLS NAICS 33591, May 2026) — government-statistical, high confidence.
- **The two-tier rule and the nameplate-vs-utilized denominator confound** — triangulated and central; every figure should carry its scope, denominator, and geography.
- **No published total-supply-chain workers/GWh benchmark exists** (re-scoped) — the paper's contribution. A *cell-operations* benchmark does exist (McKinsey ~30–40 FTE/GWh; Faraday ~180) and must be acknowledged so novelty is not overstated.
- **The subsidy–workforce link is mechanism-level** with no statutory job-count minimum; OBBBA's outcome was mixed (§45X survived with phase-out; §30D ended 9/30/25; ATVM repealed).
- **2030–2035 demand band ≈ 3,300–5,273 GWh**, with a US-driven downside.

## Most important qualifications

- **Ramp exceeds steady-state intensity only directionally**, on a nameplate basis (band separation ~72–87 vs ~125–188 w/GWh). The fitted scaling curve L(t) fails on public data (R²<0) and is a **hypothesis / methodological contribution**, not a result.
- **Intensity is geography-specific:** a Western/US floor of ~67–97 w/GWh is not a global minimum; BYD ≈ 50 vs US ≈ 315 implies a ~6× spread. The "labor = 7% of cost" figure is China-context.
- **The employment multiplier (~2.2–3.4×, bias-corrected ~1.6–2.5×) is regional and upper-bound-prone** — never stack it on the full-chain headline.
- **Construction is ~52% of storage jobs** (IREC) and transient/separable — not the prior unsourced ~36%.
- **US employment share ≈ 7–10%** (the 14% upper bound is retired); 2025 cancellations (~$11B) exceeded announcements (~$8B).
- **Composition shift toward engineering roles (ROLE-03)** is anticipated, not measured; CATL grew production staff ~50% in 2025.

## Major unresolved uncertainties

The binding gap is **utilized-capacity and single-plant time-series data**, which are operator-held. Without them, the maturity curve cannot be fitted and the ramp-decay rate cannot be estimated directly. Eight claims rest on single sources (ROLE-02, ROLE-03, the maturity curve, the global workforce range, the ~6× spread, solid-state intensity, power-as-siting-driver). The one residual causal question (Q-08) — whether infrastructure strain *binds* build-out pace — remains a hypothesis, counter-indicated by BlueOval City finishing on schedule. The highest-leverage next action is **Volta member outreach** for plant-level utilized-capacity and function-level headcount.

## Implications for the paper

The paper should lead with the **scope-and-denominator framing** as its analytical hook, present the two-tier quantitative core with explicit boundaries, and position the missing total-supply-chain benchmark as its contribution. The 40-facility dataset supports band separation robustly; the master equation should be framed as a contribution-in-progress, explaining *why* the field defaults to a single steady-state figure. Differentiate the GBA "10 million" and McKinsey "18 million" full-chain scenarios from direct-manufacturing estimates throughout.

## What should not be overstated

Do not present a single ~300 w/GWh headline, a fitted labor curve, a global automation floor, a national multiplier, or a causal strain-to-delay link. Do not cite Weng et al. for cells (it studies BEV assembly), the stale ~36% construction share, the 48,000–52,500 BLS figure, the 14% US upper bound, or aerospace/cybersecurity as method analogs. The **defensibility score (85/100) is contingent** on these Stage-4 corrections propagating into the draft.
