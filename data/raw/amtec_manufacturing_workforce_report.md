# U.S. Manufacturing Workforce: Key Findings (2025–2026)

## Workforce Size & Composition
The U.S. manufacturing sector employs approximately 12.6 million workers as of April 2026, with production and nonsupervisory roles representing roughly 70% of the total workforce. This composition reflects the industry's continued dependence on hands-on operational labor.

## Labor Availability Challenges
Manufacturing faces persistent structural labor constraints rather than cyclical disruptions. Key metrics include:

- **4.1%** average unfilled roles per manufacturer (Q1 2026)
- **26%** of manufacturers reporting vacancy rates above 5%
- **440k–510k** active job openings (Jan-Mar 2026)
- **3.3%** unemployment rate among workers previously employed in manufacturing

## Compensation Trends
Production and nonsupervisory workers earned **$30.10/hour** in April 2026—the first time this benchmark crossed the $30 threshold. Total compensation averages **$46.30/hour**, with benefits comprising approximately one-third of total labor costs.

An industry recruiter noted: "Sometimes employers use lower-tier titles to justify below-market salaries for essentially senior roles."

## Skills Over Headcount
Deloitte research indicates **over one-third** of manufacturing executives identify workforce skills as their primary talent concern. The report emphasizes that "skills-based scarcity" now outweighs pure headcount shortages, driven by accelerating investment in automation and digital operations.

## Productivity & Output
Manufacturing productivity data shows volatility: **-3.2%** (Q4 2025), rebounding to **+3.6%** (Q1 2026 preliminary), with year-over-year growth at **+1.7%**. Recent gains stem partly from declining hours worked rather than output expansion.

## Future Outlook
Approximately **43%** of manufacturers plan to increase hiring over the next 12 months, though broader employment momentum remains choppy across subsectors.
