## Extracted Quotes / Claims

> "During the ramp-up phase, factories require a high volume of engineers, QA specialists, and intensive training staff. The steady state transitions to a leaner, predominantly automated workforce focused on predictive maintenance, equipment optimization, and continuous process monitoring."

> "Labor Intensity: Up to 10× more labor-intensive per unit during the initial phase. You need heavy manual intervention, intensive debugging, and continuous line-tuning."

> "Scrap rates reduce from >30x to 5x–12x lower after maturity. Key Roles shift: Data Scientists, Advanced Robotics Technicians, and Continuous Improvement Engineers who analyze plant KPIs rather than doing physical line-work."

> "The gigafactory workforce crisis ranks as one of the largest potential threats to battery manufacturing scale-up. It affects ramp-up rate, safe performance, and economic viability over the long term. Workforce gaps slow production ramp-up timelines; the gradual increase of production relies on experienced teams gradually stabilizing processes."

> "Many employees see gigafactory jobs as temporary or unstable because of intense pressure and unclear pathways for advancement. Retention policy functions as a lever of the workforce crisis in gigafactories."

## Notes

Core document for modeling labor intensity over factory lifecycle. Documents the shift from labor-intensive ramp-up (10× multiplier) to lean, automation-dominated steady state. Scrap rate reduction (>30x initial → 5x–12x at maturity) provides quantitative anchor for yield-driven labor productivity improvements. Emphasizes critical role of workforce stability and retention during ramp-up as a constraint on production timelines and cost. Directly supports View A (labor intensity curve) development. Key insight: workforce availability/retention is a variable constraint on production scaling, not just a derived consequence.
