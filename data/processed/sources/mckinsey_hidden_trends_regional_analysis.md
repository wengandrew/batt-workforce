## Extracted Quotes / Claims

> "Although battery supply may exceed demand at the global level, the picture is more nuanced and varied by region. Some countries have excess capacity—meaning more than enough to satisfy local demand—while others rely on imports to alleviate local shortages."

> "In the base case scenario, worldwide demand is expected to rise from about 1,970 gigawatt-hours (GWh) in 2025 to around 3,910 GWh by 2030."

> "In the base case scenario, in which demand continues to grow according to the current trajectory, China's current oversupply of lithium-ion batteries—both L(M)FP and NMC—persists through 2030 even if all announced capacity materializes."

## Notes

**Authors**: Jakob Fleischmann, Martin Linder, Raphael Rettig (McKinsey Automotive & Assembly Practice), with Alexandre Van de Rijt and Christian Ahrberg  
**Published**: August 2025 (future-facing analysis as of current date 2026-06-12)  
**Affiliation**: McKinsey Center for Future Mobility

This article is the primary source for regional market-share and supply-chain positioning data feeding into the global jobs projection model (View B). It analyzes regional imbalances in battery supply vs. demand, with implications for:

1. **Market share by region (2030)**:
   - China: Persistent oversupply (dominates L(M)FP); ~64% of capacity devoted to L(M)FP chemistries
   - Europe: ~70 GWh undersupply → requires imports
   - North America: Shifts from 50 GWh undersupply (2025) → potential oversupply (2030) dependent on IRA success
   - Rest of World: Escalating undersupply, from 80 GWh (2025) to 300 GWh (2030)

2. **Key demand drivers**:
   - Global demand: 1,970 GWh (2025) → 3,910 GWh (2030) in base case
   - EV production dominates 85-90% of battery demand
   - Stationary energy storage and consumer electronics comprise remainder

3. **Supply capacity challenges**:
   - Announced nameplate capacity: 4,340 GWh (2025), 6,440 GWh (2030)
   - Realistic adjusted capacity (accounting for construction delays, ramp-up inefficiencies, yield loss): 4,750–5,270 GWh by 2030 depending on company scoring
   - Ramp-up inefficiencies identified as greatest capacity loss driver

4. **Chemistry mix implications**:
   - China: ~64% of 2030 capacity is L(M)FP (lower cost, better safety)
   - Europe/North America: Undersupply of L(M)FP; overfocus on NMC/NCA
   - Chemistry mismatch creates regional competitive pressures

**Methodology**: Bottom-up model combining company nameplate capacity announcements, production-likelihood scoring (considers company experience, IP, offtake agreements, revenue), and three supply scenarios (high-scoring companies only, average and above, all companies).

**Policy context**: IRA subsidies for North American manufacturing are flagged as critical swing variable — uncertainty around IRA implementation and U.S. tariff policy mentioned as risk to projections.

**Follow-up extraction**: Regional supply-demand tables (Exhibits 4–6) contain specific GWh figures for 2025/2030 by region and chemistry — essential for parameterizing market_share and policy_scenarios sensitivities in the global jobs model.
