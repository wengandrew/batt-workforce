## Notes

**Issued**: December 2024  
**Scope**: Comprehensive assessment of U.S. battery supply-chain progress, vulnerabilities, and federal policy priorities (2021–2024)  
**Coordination**: Authored by U.S. Department of Energy in collaboration with Treasury, Commerce, State, Interior (USGS), and DoD

This is a comprehensive Tier 1 government report on battery manufacturing investment, capacity, workforce, and policy. Key quantitative findings for workforce research:

### Investment & Job Creation (2021–2024)
- **Total announced investment**: >$150 billion in battery manufacturing sector
- **Job creation**: >100,000 manufacturing jobs across U.S.
- **Federal investment**: ~$33 billion from DoE, Treasury, EXIM, and EDA
  - DoE MESC grants: ~$5 billion to ~40 projects
  - LPO ATVM loans: $5.5 billion closed, $22 billion conditionally committed
  - EDA Tech Hubs: $21M Nevada (lithium-ion), $45M South Carolina/Georgia (grid resilience)

### Manufacturing Capacity & Deployment
- **Cell manufacturing pipeline**: >1,100 GWh/year by 2030
- **EV supply equivalent**: 11–17 million light-duty EVs (assuming 65–100 kWh packs)
- **Demand growth**: U.S. battery deployment expected to increase 6-fold from 2024–2035
- **Global demand**: 5-fold increase 2024–2035
- **EV market share of battery demand**: 85–90%

### Baseline Automotive Workforce
- **Automotive sector employment**: 13 million workers (U.S.)
- **Manufacturing-only employment**: ~1.1 million workers
- **Share of U.S. manufacturing output**: ~7% of GDP

### Workforce Constraints (Challenge 8)
Identifies "constrained workforce in key technical occupations and geographies" as critical vulnerability:
- **Construction bottleneck**: Shortage of welders, pipefitters, electricians driving up project costs and timelines
- **Operation bottleneck**: Limited supply of skilled manufacturing labor in key regions constraining production ramp-up
- **Remote location compounding factor**: Upstream/midstream processing facilities (mining, refining) often located in areas with tight labor markets

### Federal Workforce Development Response
**Goal 6: Invest in workforce development and domestic talent pipeline**
- DoE Vehicle Technologies Office & Argonne coordinating **Battery Workforce Challenge** program
- EDA funding regional Tech Hubs with workforce training integration
- Emphasis on community buy-in and coordination with labor/tribal stakeholders
- Priority for ensuring "economic benefits flow to fenceline communities"

### Supply Chain & Cost Challenges
- **Capex cost disparity**: Domestic battery facilities 2–7x more expensive to build than China/competitors
- **Capital cost drivers**: Construction trade shortages (labor), slow project delivery (2+ years vs. 6–12 months in PRC), higher baseline U.S. cost of doing business
- **China structural advantages**: State-backed cheap capital, overbuild capacity strategy, non-market policies

### Policy Instruments Referenced
- **Inflation Reduction Act (IRA)**: 45X manufacturing tax credit for battery components; 48C advanced energy credit for capex; 30D EV tax credit with content requirements
- **Section 301 tariffs**: 25% tariffs on PRC batteries/components (Sept 2024) to offset forced technology transfer
- **International coordination**: Minerals Security Partnership, G7+Australia Critical Materials & Minerals forum, IPEF Supply Chain Council

### Demand Projections
- **2024–2035 U.S. deployment**: 6-fold increase (base scenario from Argonne + BloombergNEF)
- **EV adoption driver**: Cost and performance improvements; underlying market fundamentals strengthen despite slowing EV sales from peak

**Methodology notes**: Report compiles federal investment data from energy.gov/invest tracker, awarded/selected DoE grants, conditionally committed loans, 48C tax credit recipients. Supply chain vulnerability assessment uses standard framework covering transparency, concentration, substitutability, and geopolitical risk.

**Critical follow-up**: The report flags workforce scarcity as a material risk to domestic battery manufacturing scale-up — detailed occupational demand breakdown and construction trade shortage quantification would support manufacturing_productivity and automation sensitivities.
