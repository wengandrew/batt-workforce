## Extracted Quotes / Claims

### Employment Outlook Under Different Policy Scenarios

> "Employment in the U.S. auto sector could rise by over 150,000 jobs in 2030 if: Battery electric vehicles rise to 50% of domestic sales of autos in 2030 and Due to smart policy measures, an increase in U.S. production of electric vehicle powertrain components brings U.S. manufacturers' share of the EV powertrain market up to their share of the internal combustion engine (ICE) powertrains for today's gas-powered cars, and the U.S.-assembled share of the overall vehicle market increases by 10 percentage points (to 60%)."

> "Due to manufacturing policy inaction, a rise in BEVs to 50% of domestic auto sales by 2030 could see losses of roughly 75,000 jobs by 2030. These losses would stem from policy failures that stunted investment in domestic capacity of U.S. producers to build the batteries and drivetrains of BEVs, and from a failure to regain market share in overall vehicle sales."

> "Should the U.S. fail to act on both EV deployment and manufacturing, fall further behind other nations as an EV market and location for EV production investment, and lose further market share, job losses could be even greater."

> "If a 10-percentage-point increase in overall vehicle sales market share could be matched with domestic competitive parity in producing EV and ICE powertrains, then the introduction of BEVs could lead to job gains exceeding 150,000."

### Automation and Assembly Labor Requirements

> "Based on public comments from auto industry leaders regarding the fewer hours needed to assemble EV drivetrains, we further modified the existing auto-sector model by reducing labor requirements in auto assembly by 30% per vehicle."

> "Assembly jobs are lost because BEV assembly is less complex and requires fewer person-hours than ICE assembly. The lost parts jobs are mostly due to the fact that the U.S. currently lags Europe and Asia in domestic battery and battery cell and materials production capacity, resulting in lost domestic employment in powertrains."

### Domestic Content and Powertrain Manufacturing

> "Very roughly, the current share of BEV powertrain components produced in the U.S. is just under 45%, while for ICE powertrains it is closer to 75%."

> "If policy supports increasing domestic production of EV powertrain components to levels comparable with domestic content of ICE powertrains, then a huge share of job losses embedded in the scenarios of Figure A can be avoided. All of these job-loss reductions are accomplished by reducing job losses in auto parts."

### Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle (PHEV) Role

> "PHEVs actually require more assembly jobs and slightly more parts than even ICE vehicles, hence the very substantial reduction in assembly jobs lost relative to the second scenario. PHEVs also require an ICE-type powertrain, so more of the powertrain is supplied by domestic producers and hence the job losses in parts is also blunted relative to other scenarios."

> "Interim production of plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs) alongside BEVs could significantly ameliorate sources of downward pressure on jobs as BEV penetration expands."

### Job Quality and Unionization

> "Wages in the auto sector continue to be higher than in the rest of the economy, but this relative pay premium is driven by the unionized segment of the sector, and the unionization rate in autos has declined in recent decades."

> "The share of workers covered by a collective bargaining agreement is 17.0%, compared with 11.8% in the overall workforce, 9.3% in all of manufacturing, and 7.1% in the private sector."

> "As the auto sector's high union coverage relative to coverage overall erodes rapidly in the 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s, the relative pay advantage of working in the automobile sector declines as well. In the late 1980s, auto workers were nearly three times as likely to be unionized as workers overall and earned roughly 60% higher wages at the median; in 2020, they were only slightly more likely to be unionized and had median wages just 7% higher."

> "Wages at the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles are significantly higher for workers without a four-year college degree in the unionized automobile sector. The same holds true for the shares of workers covered by employer-provided health insurance and pension plans."

### Demographic Workforce Composition

> "Black workers account for 12.5% of workers economywide, but 16.6% of workers in the auto sector, while workers without a four-year degree account for 62.2% of workers economywide but 74.6% in the auto sector."

> "The overall workforce is 51.8% male and 12.5% Black, and 24.1% have a four-year college degree as their highest level of educational attainment. In the automobile production sector, 74.2% of workers are male, 16.6% are Black, and 17.2% have a four-year college degree but not an advanced degree."

> "Automobile workers are also heavily concentrated regionally. The seven states of the Upper Midwest account for 54.8% of automobile jobs, the 11 states of the South account for 30.1% of auto jobs, California accounts for 4.5% of auto jobs, and the remaining 32 states (which includes the District of Columbia) account for just 10.6% of these jobs."

> "Black workers' share of the unionized automobile production workforce (25.5%) is substantially higher than Black workers' share of the overall workforce in the Upper Midwest (10.5%), while the share of nonunionized automobile production workers who are Black (14.8%) is substantially lower than Black workers' share of the overall workforce in the South (18.9%)."

> "Black workers, and workers with less than a bachelor's degree, are likely to have the most to gain from policy action to boost U.S. market share in electric vehicle production—and the most to lose from inaction."

### Labor Intensity by Wage Percentile

> "For workers with less than a four-year college degree: 10th-percentile wage in auto sector: $11.85 (vs. $9.62 economywide); Median wage: $18.34 (vs. $15.84 economywide); 90th-percentile wage: $32.64 (vs. $32.40 economywide). Health insurance coverage: 74.0% (vs. 52.8% economywide). Pension coverage: 47.7% (vs. 34.0% economywide)."

### Key Workforce Findings:

1. **Policy Impact on Net Employment**: The central finding is the ±150,000 job swing by 2030 based on policy choices:
   - **Positive scenario** (+150k+): 50% BEV market penetration + domestic EV powertrain content reaches ICE levels + 10 ppt domestic market share gain
   - **Negative scenario** (-75k): 50% BEV market penetration + business-as-usual (no domestic content boost, no market share recovery)
   - **Intermediate scenario** (with PHEV inclusion): 25% BEV + 25% PHEV + 50% ICE reduces losses substantially due to higher assembly and parts requirements of PHEVs

2. **Automation-Driven Assembly Job Losses**: Base assumption is 30% reduction in labor hours per vehicle for BEV assembly vs. ICE, independent of policy. This is a core structural challenge that policy cannot fully offset but can mitigate through volume gains.

3. **Domestic Content Gap**: Current U.S. production of EV powertrain components (batteries, drivetrains) is 45%, vs. 75% for ICE powertrains. This gap is the primary driver of parts job losses under business-as-usual scenarios. Policy-driven onshoring of battery and drivetrain manufacturing is the highest-leverage intervention.

4. **Regional Concentration**: 54.8% of auto jobs in Upper Midwest (Illinois, Indiana, Michigan, Missouri, Ohio, West Virginia, Wisconsin); 30.1% in South (11 states); 4.5% in California. This regional skew is critical for understanding differential policy impacts.

5. **Unionization Premium and Erosion**: Union jobs in autos pay 17% higher median wages ($20.95 vs. $17.95 for nonunion workers without college degree). Union coverage in auto sector is 17.0% (vs. 11.8% economywide), but has eroded from near 3x economywide rate in late 1980s. Wage premium has collapsed from 60% in late 1980s to 7% in 2020 as unionization declined.

6. **Workforce Demographics and Equity**: Black workers represent 16.6% of auto employment (vs. 12.5% economywide, 18.9% in South region); 74.6% of auto workers lack four-year degree (vs. 62.2% economywide). Unionized auto sector captures Black workers better (25.5%) than nonunionized (14.8%), suggesting union jobs are critical for equitable access. Policy scenarios will have disproportionate impact on low-education and Black workers.

7. **Wage Structure**: Median auto worker wage $18.34/hr (non-college educated), 16% above economywide median ($15.84). But premiums are concentrated in unionized sector; nonunion auto jobs are much closer to economy-wide averages.

8. **Temp Labor Risk**: 14% of auto production workers (as of 2014) hired via temporary staffing agencies at significantly depressed wages — hidden quality-of-life degradation not fully captured in aggregate wage statistics.

### Sensitivities Identified:

- **policy_scenarios**: Core finding; quantifies employment impact of three dimensions: (1) BEV/PHEV/ICE market share mix, (2) domestic EV powertrain content, (3) overall domestic auto market share
- **automation**: 30% labor reduction per vehicle for BEV assembly; PHEV labor premium (20% above ICE equivalents); modeling shows assembly jobs more vulnerable than parts
- **manufacturing_productivity**: IMPLAN input-output analysis capturing direct (assembly), indirect (supply chain), and induced (wage spending) employment effects; detailed for regional spillovers (not explicit in text but implicit in multi-regional framework)
- **market_share**: Explicit modeling of ±10 ppt domestic market share swings; demonstrates that regaining U.S. auto sector market position is as important as onshoring EV components
- **job_quality** (implicit, not formally named in report): Unionization differential, wage premium erosion, temporary staffing penetration; suggests labor standards / collective bargaining policy levers are as critical as manufacturing policy

### Methodology Strength:

Uses IMPLAN (2019) input-output model with detailed custom assumptions for BEV/PHEV labor requirements. Holds overall market size and cost structure constant to isolate EV manufacturing impact from macroeconomic factors. This is more conservative and focused than some studies that model broader economy-wide impacts.
