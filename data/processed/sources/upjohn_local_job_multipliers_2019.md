## Notes

**Scope and significance:**
- Upjohn Institute peer-reviewed research on economic impact multiplier bias
- Addresses a critical methodological issue in battery manufacturing economic impact studies
- Many state/regional analyses (e.g., C2ES, Ohio Governor, Georgia incentive analyses) rely on IMPLAN multipliers
- This research suggests those estimates are systematically inflated

**Key findings:**
- IMPLAN and similar I-O models assume "closed economy" within a region, which overstates local recirculation
- Actual multipliers in durable goods (battery manufacturing is durable manufacturing) are lower: 1.6-2.5x vs. 3.4x in IMPLAN
- Multiplier varies with: (1) import leakage (goods sourced from outside region), (2) labor savings rate (do local workers spend wages locally?), (3) supply-chain integration

**Implications for workforce modeling:**
- Stage-5 claim UNDR-03 cites this research to correct multiplier bias
- When state agencies claim "X direct jobs create Y total jobs," the Y figure often uses IMPLAN's 3.4x multiplier
- Reality is closer to 1.6-2.5x, meaning true local employment impact is lower
- This affects policy_scenarios sensitivity: workforce projections from C2ES and similar sources should be downward-adjusted by ~25-33%

**Cross-references:**
- Directly critiques the methodology behind c2es_research (IMPLAN ~3.4x multiplier)
- Nevada's 2.2x multiplier (nevada_goed_fy24) aligns with Upjohn's findings and is more realistic than IMPLAN
- Compare to upjohn_naatbatt_2023 (same institution) for complementary full-chain workforce analysis

**Caveats and context:**
- Publication date: 2019; may not reflect post-COVID supply-chain changes or EV-specific economic dynamics
- Focused on durable goods in US regional context; applicability to emerging battery clusters may differ
- Paper does not address Chinese battery manufacturing or foreign investment dynamics

**Methodological critique:**
- I-O models assume fixed coefficients (every $1 of output requires same input), which may understate automation effects in batteries
- Models don't account for wage volatility or skills-based matching
- Upjohn's alternative methodology: uses matched establishment data and labor market dynamics
