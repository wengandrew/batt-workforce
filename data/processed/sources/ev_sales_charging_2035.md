## Extracted Quotes / Claims

> "The stock of EVs (i.e., the number of EVs on U.S. roads) is projected to reach 78.5 million in 2035, up from 4.5 million at the end of 2023. This is more than 26 percent of the nearly 300 million total vehicles (cars and light trucks) expected to be on U.S. roads in 2035."

> "Annual sales of EVs will be nearly 12.2 million in 2035, reaching nearly 72 percent of annual total light-duty vehicle sales in 2035. In 2030, annual EV sales are projected to reach 7.7 million and account for nearly 46 percent of total light-duty vehicle sales."

> "More than 42.2 million charge ports will be needed to support the projected 78.5 million EVs that will be on U.S. roads in 2035. This includes Level 2 (L2) chargers at homes, workplaces, and in public as well as DC fast chargers (DCFC)."

> "Approximately 325,000 DCFC ports will be needed to support the level of EVs projected to be on the road in 2035."

> "America's electric companies are making significant investments to expand access to EV charging, including investing more than $5.3 billion in charging infrastructure and other EV programs."

> "Policy developments at the federal and state levels... include: Vehicle standards: The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) finalized its companion greenhouse gas regulation in March 2024 that covers model years 2027 through 2032... require increasingly stringent greenhouse gas emissions from vehicles through 2032, which are likely only to be met through the increased production and sales of EVs."

## Notes

Consensus EV demand and charging infrastructure forecast (2024–2035) prepared by Edison Electric Institute. Combines four independent expert forecasts (Guidehouse 2024, BCG 2023, PwC 2024, EY 2024) to project EV adoption trajectory and associated infrastructure requirements.

**Key demand drivers**:
- EV stock growth: 4.5M (2023) → 78.5M (2035)
- Annual sales growth: 2023 actual ~1.4M → 2030 projected 7.7M → 2035 projected 12.2M
- Market penetration: 72% of light-duty vehicle sales by 2035; 46% by 2030
- Infrastructure: 42.2M charge ports total (L2 + DCFC); 325k DCFC ports by 2035

**Policy context**:
- Inflation Reduction Act (IIJA) investment: $7.5B EV charging, $5B school buses, $5.6B transit buses
- EPA greenhouse gas standards (2027–2032) favor electrification; less stringent than originally proposed but still require EV growth
- Report explicitly notes: forecasts may not reflect most recent policy developments; potential impact of policy on EV market discussed but scenario assumes continuation of early-2025 policy environment

**Technology considerations**:
- Battery cost decline continues to drive EV cost competitiveness
- Solid-state battery development emerging as promising near-term technology

**Methodology rigor**:
- Consensus approach reduces single-source bias
- Forecasts account for customer preference models, declining battery costs, fuel efficiency standards, environmental regulations
- Missing data estimated conservatively based on trend extrapolation

**Critical caveat**: 
Report notes that "forecasts provided by each of the sources... were the most up-to-date available, but due to the rapidly evolving market and regulatory landscape, forecasts may not reflect the most recent developments as of the publication date." This report published Oct 2024; recent policy changes (2026) may have moderated projections.

**Follow-up extraction ideas**:
- Charge port deployments by state/region could inform regional labor intensity modeling
- Charging infrastructure construction workforce (electricians, technicians, installers) not explicitly covered but implied by ~325k DCFC ports
- Annual deployment rate extrapolation (2026–2035) could inform manufacturing capacity ramp schedules and associated workforce ramp requirements
- Policy scenario sensitivities: full EV adoption (72% by 2035) vs. moderated scenarios post-2025 policy changes
