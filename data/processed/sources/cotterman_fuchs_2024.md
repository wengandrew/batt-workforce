**Labor-hour to workers conversion:**
- 14-22 labor-hours/GWh estimate requires assumptions about:
  - Work hours per employee per year (e.g., 1,920 hours/year = 40 hrs/week * 48 weeks)
  - Overhead allocation (indirect labor, supervision, support)
- Rough conversion: 20 labor-hours/GWh * (1,000,000 GWh) / (1,920 hrs/yr) ≈ 10.4M labor-hours ≈ 5,400 workers for 1,000 GWh globally
- This converts to ~54 workers/GWh (5.4M workers / 100 GWh), suggesting lower bound vs. empirical data

**What drives the range (14-22):**
- Dry-electrode processes (15-20 hours/GWh) vs. wet coating (18-22 hours/GWh)
- Automation level (highly automated → lower hours)
- Process configuration (continuous vs. batch)
- Facility maturity (mature plants < ramp-up plants due to scrap/rework reduction)

**Cross-references:**
- 14-22 labor-hours converts to ~54-79 workers/GWh (using 1,920 hrs/yr baseline), bracketing the empirical steady-state (72-87 w/GWh) and bottom-up estimates (64-68 w/GWh)
- Suggests Cotterman & Fuchs provides a middle-ground estimate between engineering models and empirical facility data

**Implications:**
- Recent peer-reviewed analysis confirms labor intensity estimates in the 54-79 worker/GWh range
- Dry-electrode process improvements could reduce labor by ~15% (low end of range)
- Supports finding that steady-state ~80 workers/GWh is reasonable global benchmark
