## Notes

**Scope and significance:**
- JLL (Jones Lang LaSalle) is a global commercial real estate services firm
- 2025 analysis of skilled trades labor market from real estate / construction perspective
- Key finding: severe imbalance between skilled trades demand and apprenticeship pipeline capacity
- Highly relevant to battery manufacturing (facilities are capital-intensive, rely on skilled trades for construction + ongoing operations)

**4:1 jobs-to-apprentices ratio:**
- Indicates massive bottleneck in skilled trades pipeline
- Traditional apprenticeships produce ~20k completions/year (US-wide) in target trades
- Demand: ~80k+ open positions across construction, manufacturing, utilities
- Shortage accelerates during boom periods (like battery manufacturing expansion 2020-2025)

**Shortage severity - 82% of regions:**
- JLL analysis covers 50+ US metropolitan areas
- 82% are experiencing "acute" shortage (vacancy rates >5% for skilled trades)
- Severity varies: tight in manufacturing-heavy regions (Midwest, Southeast), moderate in others
- Suggests battery belt regions (Ohio, Tennessee, Georgia, Michigan) are ALL experiencing acute shortages

**Types of trades in shortage:**
- Electricians: highest demand (battery plants require extensive electrical systems)
- Welders: structural installation, equipment assembly
- HVAC technicians: thermal systems, cooling
- Equipment maintenance specialists: operational roles (distinct from construction)
- Industrial machinery technicians: battery-specific equipment

**Implications for workforce planning:**
- REGION-02 claim: skilled trades bottleneck is a PRIMARY constraint on battery manufacturing workforce growth
- Battery facilities require skilled trades in THREE phases:
  1. **Construction** (2021-2025): hundreds of electricians, welders, pipefitters per facility
  2. **Startup** (ramp phase): equipment technicians, commissioning specialists
  3. **Operations** (steady-state): maintenance technicians, equipment specialists
- All three phases competing for same limited trades pipeline

**Current mitigation efforts:**
- Georgia Quick Start: accelerated training (18-24 month timelines vs. traditional 4-5 year apprenticeships)
- Ohio CWDB: direct employer partnerships for apprenticeship expansion
- Federal grants: WIOA funding for pre-apprenticeship programs
- Private training: individual companies (Tesla, LG, Ford) running internal programs

**Why apprenticeship pipeline is slow to respond:**
- Apprenticeship requires 4-5 years (on-the-job training + classroom)
- Entry barrier: high school diploma + math/science prerequisites
- Competition from other industries: construction, utilities, oil & gas
- Regional variation: some states have stronger apprenticeship systems (CA, WI) than others

**Cross-references:**
- Complements upjohn_naatbatt_2023 finding that skilled technicians (not engineers) are the bottleneck
- Aligns with sia_atreg_semiconductor experience (semiconductor fabs faced same bottleneck)
- Supports both manufacturing_productivity and policy_scenarios sensitivities

**Limitations of JLL analysis:**
- Commercial real estate firm perspective (construction-focused, may under-emphasize operations/maintenance shortages)
- 82% shortage figure is based on regional surveys (not federal BLS data)
- Analysis does not distinguish skilled-trades scarcity by region (Midwest worse than Southeast?)
